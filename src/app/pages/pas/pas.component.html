OPAS Case Study

An enterprise grade application for managing workflows.

Key Info

Details will likely be vague to protect client info.

Overview & Problems

The original Process Automation System was deployed in 2006, using an antiquated low-code no-code solution, that was meant to manage a variety of workflow types. The technology used to build it made development fast but rigid and, as a result, the solution could not be optimized for user experience. The years following the original deployment were unresponsive to users’ feedback due to technology constraints. This led to long, frustrating, time consuming experiences for users.

In 2016, a new version of the application was approved to be built. The organization knew they wanted to create an experience that reduced the time users spent to complete tasks. However, it was now clear after years of feedback, the primary user was not obvious. Users had drastically different goals in the application. Some were approvers and others were submitters with varying expertise in the previous solution.

To design and build a successful replacement application, it had to be flexible to user needs, address radically different user goals and minimize the time spent to complete tasks.

Goals

  • Design and build a modern web application to replace the existing application
  • Create a solution that is flexible enough to provide a positive experience for users with widely differing objectives
  • Build a templatized UI that dynamically loads content and generates pages from JSON data without the need for deployments for content changes
  • Engineer an experience to help novice users identify forms that are required to be completed based on user scenario – travel, expense, security reporting, etc.
  • Develop two unique experiences (novice, expert) for users that promotes timeliness, achieved with familiar industry design patterns and contextual content visibility for convenience
  • Create an inclusive application for users with disabilities in compliance with 508 requirements

Project Scope

Replacement of existing application

Design Process

01/Research

Meet Users

My design process starts and end with users in mind. When designing the new OPAS application I wanted to speak directly to users to understand how the current application was failing and succeeding. During this process it was clear that the perceived user base and actual user base were quite different. It was previously assumed that most users were high frequency users. However, power users were a smaller percentage of users than anticipated. After meeting with users, it was apparent lower frequency (novice) users were 50%+ of the user base and the application needed to better accommodate them.

Methods

  • Interviews – 30+ participants
  • Card sorting
  • Field studies – 5 disabled users (508 compliance)
  • Surveys – 300~ responses

Baseline

To understand where we were heading, we needed to know what we were coming from. Users had complained that the old solution made find forms difficult, but we need to know why this process was difficult. Usability testing helped us discover that forms names, groupings and locations were confusing. A form named TR-251 was harder to digest than International Travel. Additionally, we observed there was a fear of opening a form and realizing it was the wrong one. We needed to tell the user clearly what a forms function was.

Additionally, the division that owned OPAS could not communicate with users to notify them of future outages, updates and changes using the existing application. Instead, these notifications had to be communicated over email. The new application also had to give the owners of OPAS the ability to tell users the status of the application.

Methods

  • Usability Testing – 5 participants (novice) + 5 participants (power)
  • Surveys – 300 responses

Competitive Analysis

Rather than starting from scratch, I looked to other solutions that already existed to understand how they approached the problem of creating an experience for submitters (novice) and approvers (power). Some low code/no code solutions provided unique UIs for different users but were rigid and difficult to customize. Other solutions tried to accommodate both users with similar experiences but ultimately delivered a watered-down design that appeased neither user base. However, these solutions appeared to be more flexible.

The new OPAS application had to be robust enough to work with many technologies and flexible enough to provide a unique experience for each user. We needed a hybrid solution.

Methods

  • Interviews
  • Card sorting
  • Field studies
  • Surveys

02/Strategy

Personas

Personas allowed me to summarize a large user base into a few segments and then design solutions around these. I also used personas to familiarize the team with our users. So, instead of talking about users abstractly, we now could better empathize with them. The team needed to know that our decisions impact real people and not just a user id in a database.

Insights

  • Developers cared about a user's exprience more than I anticipated
  • User stories became easier to write and more impactful

Customer Journey Map

Creating a customer journey map allowed me to put myself in the shoes of the user and identify pain points and joyful moments experienced during the process of completing a user goal. Then, after identifying these points, I worked to remediate bad experiences and maximize good ones.

The customer journey map made finding opportunities for improvement clear and led to an experience that was better for users. Often, the improvements were derived from common design practices rather than custom, one-off solutions.

Insights

  • The problems are often not unique to OPAS, look for common solutions
  • Users go through a rollercoaster of emotions while completing tasks – let's minimize this
  • Identifying ownership of problems is't always clear

Save research for the true unknowns.

NN/g

Usability Heurisitcs

While most designs for OPAS were custom, all of them were formulated with consideration to NN/g 10 Usability Heuristics. I wanted to maximize my time spent on solving truly unique problems experienced by OPAS users, rather than waste time researching, and creating novel designs. As a result, most designs were fundamentally sound and only required small tweaks.

Outcomes

  • Two unique expriences tailored to its perspective audience
  • Overhaul of form names to 'speak' the users language
  • Reduction of information on the page; clear hierarchy of information
  • Most actions taken in OPAS could now be easily reversed

Common Design Standards

OPAS might have been overhauled but I still wanted the experience to be recognizable, even if users had never seen it before. The goal was to reduce cognitive load of users and promote action. To accomplish this, standards such as a logo in the top left (doubled as home page navigation), a search box and main navigation in the header and more were implemented. It’s estimated that up to 93% of these practices are familiar to users and reduce confusion when used correctly.

Insights

  • Users don't want to learn something new
  • Flashy style wears off quickly, functional design lasts
  • Focus design efforts on unique problems
