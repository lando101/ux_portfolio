OPAS Case Study

An enterprise grade application for managing workflows.

Key Info

Details will likely be vague to protect client info.

Overview & Problems

The original Process Automation System was deployed in 2006, using an antiquated low-code no-code solution, that was meant to manage a variety of workflow types. The technology used to build it made development fast but rigid and, as a result, the solution could not be optimized for user experience. The years following the original deployment were unresponsive to users’ feedback due to technology constraints. This led to long, frustrating, time consuming experiences for users.

In 2016, a new version of the application was approved to be built. The organization knew they wanted to create an experience that reduced the time users spent to complete tasks. However, it was now clear after years of feedback, the primary user was not obvious. Users had drastically different goals in the application. Some were approvers and others were submitters with varying degrees of expertise.

To design and build a successful replacement application, it had to be flexible to user needs, address radically different user goals and minimize the time spent to complete tasks.

Goals

  • Design and build a modern web application to replace the existing application
  • Create a solution that is flexible enough to provide a positive experience for users with widely differing objectives
  • Build a templatized UI that dynamically loads content and generates pages from JSON data without the need for deployments for content changes
  • Engineer an experience to help novice users identify forms that are required to be completed based on user scenario – travel, expense, security reporting, etc.
  • Develop two unique experiences (novice, expert) for users that promotes timeliness and efficiency
  • Create an inclusive application for users with disabilities in compliance with 508 requirements

Design Process

01/Research

Meet Users

My design process starts and end with users in mind. When designing the new OPAS application I wanted to speak directly to users to understand how the current application was failing and succeeding. During this process, it was clear that the perceived user base and actual user base were quite different. It was previously assumed that most users were high frequency users. However, power users were a smaller percentage of users than anticipated. After meeting with users, it was apparent lower frequency (novice) users were 50%+ of the user base and the application needed to better accommodate them.

Methods

  • Interviews – 30+ participants
  • Card sorting
  • Field studies – 5 disabled users (508 compliance)
  • Surveys – 300~ responses

Baseline

To build a better solution, the team needed to know why the old application was failing. Users had complained that the old solution made finding forms difficult, but I needed to know why. Usability testing helped me discover that form names, groupings and locations were confusing. A form named TR-251 was harder to digest than International Travel. Additionally, we observed there was a fear of opening a form and realizing it was the wrong one. We needed to better communicate the function of a form.

Additionally, the division that owned OPAS could not communicate with users to notify them of future outages, updates and changes using the existing application. Instead, these notifications had to be communicated over email. The new application required the ability to notify users the status of the application.

Methods

  • Usability Testing – 5 novice participants + 5 power participants

Competitive Analysis

Before designing the application, I looked to other solutions that already existed. I wanted to understand how they solved the problems OPAS users were experiencing. Some low code/no code solutions provided unique UIs for different users but were rigid and difficult to customize. Other solutions tried to accommodate both users with similar experiences but ultimately delivered a watered-down design that appeased neither user base.

The new OPAS application had to be robust enough to work with many technologies and flexible enough to provide a unique experience for each user. We needed a hybrid solution.

Methods

  • Interviews
  • Card sorting
  • Field studies
  • Surveys

02/Strategy

Personas

I created personas to summarize a large user base into a few segments and then design solutions around these groups. I also used personas to familiarize the team with our users and help us better empathize with them. The team needed to know that our decisions impact real people and not just a user ID in a database.

Insights

  • Developers cared about a user's exprience more than I anticipated
  • User stories became easier to write and more impactful

Customer Journey Map

The customer journey map helped me put myself in the shoes of the user and identify pain points and joyful moments experienced by them while completing a task. Then, after identifying these points, I worked to remediate bad experiences and maximize good ones.

Insights

  • The problems are often not unique to OPAS, look for common solutions
  • Users go through a rollercoaster of emotions while completing tasks – let's minimize these
  • Identifying ownership of problems is't always clear

Save research for the true unknowns.

NN/g

Usability Heurisitcs

While most designs for OPAS were custom, all of them were formulated with consideration to NN/g 10 Usability Heuristics. I wanted to maximize my time spent on solving truly unique problems, rather than use time researching, and creating novel designs. As a result, most designs were fundamentally sound and only required small tweaks.

Outcomes

  • Two unique expriences tailored to its perspective audience
  • Overhaul of form names to 'speak' the users language
  • Reduction of information on the page; clear hierarchy of information
  • Most actions taken in OPAS could now be easily reversed

Common Design Standards

OPAS might have been overhauled but I still wanted the experience to be recognizable, even if users had never seen it before. The goal was to reduce cognitive load and promote action. To accomplish this, standards such as a logo in the top left (doubled as home page navigation), a search box and main navigation in the header and more were implemented. It’s estimated that up to 93% of these practices are familiar to users and reduce confusion when used correctly.

Insights

  • Users don't want to learn something new
  • Flashy style wears off quickly, functional design lasts
  • Focus design efforts on unique problems

03/Design

Site Map

OPAS was, functionally speaking, a large application, but its page count was relatively low. This is highlighted by the site map. The site map helped me organize the application and decide where to place content. I found myself coming back to this visualization when I needed to mentally shrink the scope of OPAS.

Insights

  • A birds eye view simplifies the application
  • A high level view of the application made re-organizing and cutting pages easier

Task Flow

I used task flow diagrams to ensure processes in OPAS were sequential. Each step in completing a task was intended to feel necessary and avoided “leaps of faith” or guessing. The task flow diagram helped me view a task from a user’s perspective and eliminate or add steps that would make a task easier to complete.

Outcomes

  • Clear indicators were added when launching a form that left OPAS
  • Some tasks required more clicks but lead to less errors and higher completion rates for users

Mockups

01/Sketches

Before jumping into tools such as Adobe XD or Figma, I like to slow the design process down. Starting on pencil and paper gives me a moment to collect my thoughts and begin designing without the distractions of notifications and errors. I find, after completing sketches, my mid-fidelity mock-ups are focused and materialize faster.

02/Mid-fidelity Mockup

For me, mid-fidelity mock-ups are a time to organize content and further solidify page layouts. Often, my mid-fidelity mock-ups vary in completion. The more unknowns there are, the more placeholders, lorem ipsum text and gaps there are. Overtime, these mock-ups are refined and eventually reflect the mock-ups below. The absence of color is intentional, so team members and users are focused on layout and content, rather than styles.

Once mid-fidelity mock-ups have been refined, I like to show them to users. Typically, this is in the form of usability testing, which results in great feedback that is easy to implement. Refining mock-ups is always easier than re-development.

04/Prototype & Test

Styles & Branding

The OPAS style tile was a quick reference for OPAS colors, fonts, iconography and more. It documented the official styles to be used for OPAS and other future applications built by the division.

There were a wide variety of colors that were used, some more than others. ‘Area’ colors were used to help users associate types of forms with topics. These colors were eye grabbing but were used limitedly to avoid desensitizing users to them.

The style tile was used to inform style and branding of the high-fidelity mock-ups below.

High-fidelity Mock-up

High-fidelity mock-ups are one of the final steps before reaching a working prototype of the application. The OPAS high-fidelity mock-ups were used to show team members and users imagery of what the application could look like in its final form, with colors, branding, content and information hierarchies.

When creating the mock-ups, I tried to make them look as close to the real application as possible. This helps eliminate surprises and provides developers with a clear vision of what the product should look like.

While high-fidelity mock-ups clarify design decisions, they often lack detail about interactions, animations and feel. I use ‘mini’ prototypes to clarify these questions. There are a few examples in the next section.

'Mini' Prototypes

I find the best way to convey the behavior of a UI is by prototyping working versions of them. On the OPAS project, I would often design interactions using Codepen and share them with developers. Frequently the HTML and SCSS I produced were used in the code base.

Primary & Secondary Buttons

Primary and secondary buttons in the high-fidelity mock-ups appeared to be static buttons that did not respond to user interaction. In the Codepen project, the buttons respond to mouse hovering by the user. I, also, mentioned when to use each respective button. Buttons were not an artistic design choice but rather a functional design decision.

Layout Dropdown

The layout drop down was intended to allow the user to customize the Task List page layout. Each option in the drop down had a label and small illustration of what the layout would look like. Although this feature did not make it to production, it was a quick way to build a working version of the drop down, and subsequently eliminate it without deploying it.

Icon Buttons

Early in the design process of the OPAS application, it was planned to remove text where possible and replace it with icons. However, we still wanted this text to be available when a user interacted with the icons. As a result, this Codepen project shows how icons would lift to reveal text that described the button's action.

Ultimately, we chose to always show text because many of the icons were not ubiquitous enough to describe the action they represented.

Layout Behaviors

The development team had varying opinions about the Task List page layout. I used Codepen to create three options and test them with users. Creating these prototypes in Codepen shorted the process of testing and resulted in a design that reflected what users wanted. Prototyping the options avoided using developer time and provided them with usable code.

Usability Testing

I frequently usability tested the OPAS application when new functionality was added. It was important to me that OPAS designs were validated by users. The high frequency of usability testing prevented the application from deviating from user needs and sanity checked our design thinking.

01/Preperation

Before conducting usability testing, I created a script and outlined what aspects of the application needed to be tested. Typically, these were new functionalities that had not been tested before. Then, I would create open ended tasks that would test each functionality. The tasks were based on goals a user might attempt in their day-to-day while using the application. Tasks never prescribed how to complete it. Additionally, each task was expected to be completed with a low error rate (<5%).

Often the note takers that participated in usability testing were not familiar with the application. Therefore, each task also included a section that highlighted what a note taker should be aware of and what metrics they should capture.

02/Conduct

Usability testing was usually scheduled with 5 users and lasted between 30 to 45 minutes. I wanted to keep testers engaged and respect their time. After all, our users had full time jobs. During usability testing, users typically completed between 10 to 20 tasks and were asked to think out loud. Periodically I reminded testers they weren’t being tested; the application was. I wanted to reinforce the fact that testers were testing the application and not the other way around.

At the end of usability testing, I thanked the user for their time and stopped the recording. Recordings were used to highlight successes and pain points experienced by users.

03/Report

After usability testing was completed, I would summarize the results into a document. The report contained both successes and failures of the current design and outlined potential ways to remediate problems. The report was used to socialize UX issues to the team and explain where and why users failed certain tasks. I wanted to keep team members looped in with the UX process so they understood my decisions.
